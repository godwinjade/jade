Contraception Methods

There are many safe and effective birth control options available today. Understanding the different methods can help you choose what's best for your body and lifestyle.

Birth Control Pills

91-99% effective with perfect use

Oral contraceptives contain hormones that prevent ovulation. They are taken daily at the same time for maximum effectiveness.

Benefits

  • Highly effective when taken correctly
  • Can regulate periods and reduce cramps
  • Doesn't interfere with sex
  • May improve acne

Considerations

  • Requires daily commitment
  • May cause side effects like nausea or mood changes
  • Doesn't protect against STIs
  • Need a prescription

Contraceptive Injection

94% effective with typical use

The contraceptive shot (Depo-Provera) is given every 3 months and contains progestin to prevent ovulation.

Benefits

  • Only needed 4 times per year
  • Highly discreet
  • Doesn't require daily attention

Considerations

  • May cause irregular bleeding initially
  • Possible weight gain
  • Return to fertility may be delayed
  • Doesn't protect against STIs

IUD (Intrauterine Device)

99% effective

A small T-shaped device inserted into the uterus by a healthcare provider that provides long-term birth control (3-10 years depending on type).

Benefits

  • Long-lasting protection
  • Highly effective immediately
  • No daily maintenance
  • Some types may reduce menstrual bleeding

Considerations

  • Requires clinic visit for insertion/removal
  • May cause cramping during insertion
  • Possible irregular bleeding initially
  • Doesn't protect against STIs

Barrier Methods

72-88% effective (condoms), 71-86% (diaphragm)

Physical barriers that prevent sperm from reaching the egg, such as condoms, diaphragms, and cervical caps.

Benefits

  • Condoms protect against STIs
  • No hormones involved
  • Immediately reversible
  • Available without prescription

Considerations

  • Must be used correctly every time
  • Can interrupt spontaneity
  • May require advance preparation (e.g., diaphragm insertion)

Comparing Effectiveness

Birth Control Effectiveness Chart

Contraceptive effectiveness is typically measured by two standards:

  • Perfect use: How effective the method is when used exactly as directed all the time
  • Typical use: How effective the method is during actual use (includes human error)

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the most effective birth control method? +

Long-acting reversible contraceptives (LARCs) like IUDs and contraceptive implants are the most effective reversible methods, with effectiveness rates over 99%. They don't depend on user compliance once they're inserted.

Can I get pregnant if I miss one pill? +

Missing one pill slightly increases your risk, especially if it's late by more than 24 hours. Always refer to your pill's specific instructions. If you miss more than one pill or are unsure, use backup contraception for 7 days and consult your healthcare provider.

Do birth control methods affect future fertility? +

Most birth control methods don't affect long-term fertility. After stopping, your body typically returns to its normal fertility level within a few months. The exception may be the contraceptive injection (Depo-Provera), which can sometimes delay the return to fertility for up to a year after stopping.

At what age can I start using birth control? +

There's no minimum age for contraceptive use. Teens can safely use hormonal birth control if they've started menstruating. Many providers recommend birth control for teens for reasons beyond pregnancy prevention, such as regulating periods or treating acne.
